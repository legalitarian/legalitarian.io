What we do

Empower humans

Simple summaries of the law and links to the full text allow people to understand their rights and defend themselves from being taken advantage of.

Bridge the gap between the people and local government

With the click of a button, residents can submit information directly to their local government.

All this information is available to government staff in a centralized location - without needing to answer the phone or keep track of piles of paper notes.

User-Centered Products

It's expensive to build a product instead of solving a problem because nothing is one-size-fits-all when it comes to the challenges local governments face. By interviewing stakeholders of a real-world problem, we identify the core goals of any project before development begins.

This process is called User Discovery, and it's crucial to addressing a need at its root in collaboration with people and their local governments.

Legalitarian products are available in multiple languages to meet the needs of diverse populations.

Budget-responsive design

We shrink the project to fit your budget while preserving your core goals.

Start addressing your needs with a project that fits your budget. With insights from User Discovery, we begin with the simplest solutions to get the most value out of the least effort, minimizing your risk.

Iterative design and development allows us to start, stop, pause, and build on the foundation as your budget and needs change over time. Grow your project at your own pace and achieve incremental success

Phone-optimized and Easy-to-use Websites

Legalitarian products load lightning fast and they're available on virtually all devices browsing the interent today.* People want to access information their way - on their phones, tablets, and computers. State-of-the-art or yesterday's news, your product is at their fingertips in an instant!

With user-first design, Legalitarian products just work. There are no hidden menus, broken pages, or overcomplicated interfaces for users to learn. Simple and intuitive pages give people the information they need and let them submit their information and get on with their day.

* based on statistics from Can I Use

Accessibility

At Legalitarian, we believe the internet should be accessible to everyone regardless of ability, and we build our products to be accessible to all. Whether you use a keyboard or voice-to-text, a screen or a screen reader, large text or small, Legalitarian is building products you can use to the fullest extent. More information can be found in our accessibility statement.

Who we are

Hi, I'm Ryan Heisler, the Chief Advocate for People and Technology at Legalitarian.

My passion is harnessing the power of the internet to help communities tackle the diverse challenges they face. The most important part of building software is not writing codeâ€”it's talking to people. I've spent my career as a software developer and consultant building relationships with my clients, their stakeholders, and the end-users of their software. By building relationships with them I'm able to get them to open up to me about the challenges they face, and then address those challenges without wasting time and effort on software that won't help them.

Before my career began, I traveled the world learning new languages so I could connect with people and learn about their lives. I'm still looking for opportunities to do that, but now I know that there are worlds of people right here in Michigan. I want to get out in the community and talk to people, and use my interpersonal and technical skills to bridge the gap between the people and their local government and help them collaborate to address the real issues they're facing.

Southeast Michigan is my community and my home, and I'm already working to build career-long partnerships that benefit the people of this region. Over 7 years, many iterations, and a rotating team of volunteers, I developed a resource to help residents of Washtenaw County use their county ID cards. It has information for them to learn about the laws surrounding the ID, advocate for themselves, and submit complaints directly to the Ann Arbor Human Rights Commission when a business refuses to accept it. The ID is a critical resource for so many people in the county, and when I'm committed to addressing a problem like that, I don't quit.
